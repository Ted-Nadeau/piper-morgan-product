## Research Artifact: UX for AI Reconnaissance Report

### Field Assessment

The field is characterized by:
- Rapid practitioner experimentation
- Fragmented pattern documentation
- Academic research struggling to keep pace with deployed products
- **Three distinct knowledge clusters** that rarely cite each other despite addressing identical challenges

### Key Thinkers Identified

**Tier 1: Essential Voices**
- **Dan Saffer** (CMU HCII) - "Designing with AI" methodology, matchmaking AI capabilities to design problems
- **Greg Nudelman** (UXforAI.com) - Most systematic practitioner pattern work (35 AI projects documented)
- **Jakob Nielsen** (UX Tigers) - "Articulation barrier" vocabulary, prompt engineering as UX failure
- **Victor Dibia** (Microsoft AutoGen) - Agent UX framework with four principles

**Tier 2: Company Design Teams**
- Microsoft Copilot (three altitudes framework)
- Google Gemini
- Anthropic Claude (chatbot to work platform transformation)
- Perplexity

### Eight Thematic Clusters Identified

| Cluster | Maturity | Core Tension |
|---------|----------|--------------|
| **1. Trust & Calibration** | HIGH | Avoiding algorithm aversion AND automation complacency |
| **2. Conversation vs. Artifact** | HIGH | Claude Artifacts vs. OpenAI Canvas paradigms |
| **3. Agency & "Who's Driving"** | MEDIUM | System-initiated vs. user-initiated delegation |
| **4. Memory & Continuity** | MEDIUM-HIGH | What AI remembers, decay, user control |
| **5. Prompt Articulation** | HIGH | Nielsen's "articulation barrier" for low-articulation users |
| **6. Agent & Autonomous UX** | HIGH but NASCENT | OpenAI Operator, mission-control interfaces |
| **7. Non-determinism** | MEDIUM | Is determinism even desirable? |
| **8. Multi-modal Integration** | LOW-MEDIUM | Most underexplored relative to market importance |

### Key Research Findings

**On Trust Calibration** (Zhang et al.):
- Confidence scores help calibrate trust but don't automatically improve decisions
- Local explanations (feature importance) had **no effect** on trust calibration

**On Agency** (Adam et al., 2024):
- When AI offers to take over (system-initiated delegation) vs. user asking, users feel "self-threat" and resist more
- Less perceived post-delegation control = more resistance

**On Articulation** (Nielsen):
- People struggle to express intent in writing → "low-articulation users"
- Prompt engineering is a UX failure, not a user failure

**IDEO Finding**:
- **56% more ideas** with AI-generated questions
- **28% decrease** with AI-generated example ideas
- Questions scaffold; examples constrain

### Critical Gaps Identified

- **Longitudinal studies**: Nearly all research uses single-session evaluations
- **Cross-platform consistency**: Almost no research on designing consistent AI experiences across devices
- **Practitioner-academic disconnect**: Rarely cite each other despite addressing identical challenges
- **AI personality design**: Beyond voice/tone, how to design consistent AI "character" over time

## Key Insights Crystallized

### 1. Settings = Abdication
Every setting is admission the product team couldn't decide. Have a point of view. Lead with expertise.

### 2. Yoga Class Principle
Instructors don't present settings panels to beginners. They have informed opinions about what novices need.

### 3. Trust Gradient as Architecture
Not metaphor but actual system design. How trust earns, how it's lost, how it's displayed.

### 4. Canonical Queries as Recognition Interface
If users struggle to articulate (the barrier is real), Piper articulates and user selects. "Did you mean..." becomes primary pattern.

### 5. Dreaming as Background Processing
Two types: filing (cross-reference, unexpected connections) and anxiety (risk simulation). A colleague thinks about the work even when you're not there.

### 6. Who's Driving is Load-Bearing
Research shows system-initiated delegation creates resistance. How does Piper offer without threatening? This is a central design challenge.
