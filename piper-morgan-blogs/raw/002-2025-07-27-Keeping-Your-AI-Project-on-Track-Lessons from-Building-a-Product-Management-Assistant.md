    <p id="ember340" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <em><!---->On flashback weekends I will share process pieces drawn from my experience building Piper Morgan. This is the first one I wrote, orginally for my team at Kind. I’ve revised it slightly and should note that our processes have evolved a great deal since June 14, but this was the foundation of the rigor to come.<!----></em>
<!---->    </p>

<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!----><!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

  <hr class="reader-divider-block__horizontal-rule">


<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember341" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <em><!---->June 14, 2025<!----></em>
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember342" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <em><!---->A guide for product and UX professionals working with technical teams on AI initiatives<!----></em>
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember343" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <!---->So there I was two weeks into building an AI PM assistant, watching my technical team of bots explain why yesterday’s working features had mysteriously broken overnight. Again.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember344" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <!---->The symptoms were familiar to anyone who’s shipped products: scope creep disguised as “quick wins,” technical debt accumulating faster than features shipped, and that sinking feeling that we were building something impressive that nobody could actually use.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember345" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <!---->But the underlying patterns were different from traditional product work. AI development has its own failure modes, its own hidden complexities, and its own ways of making smart teams look foolish. Here’s what I learned about keeping AI projects on track, especailly when you’re not the one writing the code.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <h2 id="ember346" class="ember-view reader-text-block__heading-2">
      <!---->The “quick win” trap that isn’t quick<!---->
<!---->    </h2>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember347" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <!---->When we started building Piper Morgan, every implementation decision was about getting something built rapidly. Make the API call work? Just hardcode the URL for now. Get the workflow executing? Skip the error handling, we’ll add it later. Store user data? A simple JSON file will do.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember348" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <strong><!---->Each shortcut felt minor. Together, they created architectural debt that slowed everything down.<!----></strong>
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember349" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <!---->This is like designing a user flow without checking your design system. Individual decisions seem harmless, but they compound into inconsistent experiences that confuse users and slow development. The AI equivalent is making technical decisions without understanding how they’ll integrate with your actual product requirements.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember350" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <strong><!---->For Product Teams<!----></strong><!---->: When your technical team says “this will just be a quick fix” or “we can patch this for now,” ask about the long-term impact. In AI systems, quick fixes often create problems that are expensive to solve later because AI components are more interconnected than traditional software.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember351" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <strong><!---->What to Watch For<!----></strong><!---->: Implementation shortcuts that bypass your domain logic or business rules. These might work for demos but fail when real users try to accomplish actual tasks.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <h2 id="ember352" class="ember-view reader-text-block__heading-2">
      <!---->The multi-environment reality check<!---->
<!---->    </h2>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember353" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <!---->Here’s a practical problem that reveals deeper issues: we kept losing development momentum because the system worked on one machine but not another. What seemed like a minor DevOps annoyance actually highlighted that our system wasn’t operationally mature.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember354" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <strong><!---->For Product Teams<!----></strong><!---->: This is equivalent to designers not being able to open each other’s files or having different component libraries. If your technical team is spending significant time on environment setup rather than building features, it signals architectural problems that will affect product quality.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember355" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <strong><!---->The deeper issue<!----></strong><!---->: AI systems often depend on external services, model versions, and configuration that can drift between environments. Unlike traditional software that behaves predictably, AI systems can fail in subtle ways that are hard to debug.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember356" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <strong><!---->What to Ask<!----></strong><!---->: “How long does it take a new team member to get a working development environment?” If the answer is more than 30 minutes, your team needs to invest in operational consistency before building more features.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <h2 id="ember357" class="ember-view reader-text-block__heading-2">
      <!---->Domain-first architecture: Why business logic should drive technical decisions<!---->
<!---->    </h2>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember358" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <!---->Our breakthrough came when we realized we’d created three different definitions of the same business concept (“Task”) across different parts of the system. Each made sense locally, but they couldn’t work together.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember359" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <strong><!---->The Business Parallel<!----></strong><!---->: Imagine if your marketing team, sales team, and product team all defined “customer” differently. You’d have integration problems, inconsistent experiences, and data that doesn’t connect.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember360" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <strong><!---->For Product Teams<!----></strong><!---->: When working with AI systems, insist that business concepts — workflows, users, tasks — remain consistent across all technical implementations. This isn’t just cleaner code; it enables features that work across your entire product.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember361" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <strong><!---->The AI-specific challenge<!----></strong><!---->: AI systems often create their own internal representations of your business concepts. If these diverge from your actual domain model, you end up with features that work in isolation but can’t integrate with real workflows.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember362" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <strong><!---->What to Demand<!----></strong><!---->: Your technical team should be able to explain how business concepts map to their implementation. If they can’t, or if the same concept is implemented differently in different places, you have architecture drift that will slow future development.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <h2 id="ember363" class="ember-view reader-text-block__heading-2">
      <!---->Set up a learning loop<!---->
<!---->    </h2>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember364" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <!---->AI systems aren’t like traditional software that works the same way every time. They need to improve through use, which means building feedback collection from day one — not as an afterthought.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember365" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <strong><!---->For Product Teams<!----></strong><!---->: This is like launching a product without analytics. You might have initial success, but you can’t improve without understanding how users actually interact with your system.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember366" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <strong><!---->The systematic approach<!----></strong><!---->: Every AI feature should include mechanisms for users to indicate whether the output was helpful. But this isn’t just thumbs up/down — you need structured feedback that helps the system learn specific improvements.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember367" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <strong><!---->What to Plan For<!----></strong><!---->: Feedback loops that capture not just “was this right?” but “what would have been better?” AI systems that can’t learn from user corrections will plateau quickly and frustrate users.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <h2 id="ember368" class="ember-view reader-text-block__heading-2">
      <!---->Technical infrastructure enables product vision<!---->
<!---->    </h2>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember369" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <!---->Getting database persistence working wasn’t just a technical milestone — it enabled the product vision of a system that learns and remembers. Without proper data storage, AI systems can’t build on previous interactions or maintain context across sessions.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember370" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <strong><!---->For Product Teams<!----></strong><!---->: This is like building a personalization system without user profiles. You might have impressive individual interactions, but you can’t create the cumulative value that makes AI assistants truly useful.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember371" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <strong><!---->The compounding effect<!----></strong><!---->: AI systems become more valuable over time as they accumulate context and learn user preferences. But this only works if the technical foundation supports persistent state and learning.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember372" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <strong><!---->What to Validate<!----></strong><!---->: Ensure your technical team can demonstrate that the system maintains state and learns from interactions. If every conversation starts from scratch, you’re building a sophisticated chatbot, not an intelligent assistant.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <h2 id="ember373" class="ember-view reader-text-block__heading-2">
      <!---->Demo-safe development: Managing AI’s inherent unpredictability<!---->
<!---->    </h2>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember374" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <!---->AI development is inherently experimental. Features that work in testing might fail during a stakeholder demo because AI responses can vary in ways that traditional software doesn’t.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember375" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <strong><!---->For Product Teams<!----></strong><!---->: This is like maintaining a stable prototype while iterating on new concepts. You need to balance innovation with reliability, especially when showing progress to stakeholders who might not understand AI’s probabilistic nature.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember376" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <strong><!---->The practical solution<!----></strong><!---->: Your technical team should maintain a “demo-ready” version that always works, separate from active development. This prevents the embarrassment of features breaking during important presentations.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember377" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <strong><!---->What to Require<!----></strong><!---->: Separate branches or versions for demonstrations that use deterministic responses or well-tested scenarios. Innovation happens in development; reliability happens in demos.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <h2 id="ember378" class="ember-view reader-text-block__heading-2">
      <!---->Questions product leaders should ask<!---->
<!---->    </h2>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <h3 id="ember379" class="ember-view reader-text-block__heading-3">
      <!---->About architecture health<!---->
<!---->    </h3>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember380" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <strong><!---->“Can you show me how our business concepts map to your technical implementation?”<!----></strong><span class="white-space-pre"> </span>If they can’t draw clear lines from your domain model to their code, you have architecture drift.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember381" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <strong><!---->“What happens when we need to add a new workflow type?”<!----></strong><span class="white-space-pre"> </span>This reveals whether they’ve built extensible patterns or hardcoded solutions.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember382" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <strong><!---->“How long would it take to integrate with a new external system?”<!----></strong><span class="white-space-pre"> </span>Tests whether they’ve built abstractions that prevent vendor lock-in.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <h3 id="ember383" class="ember-view reader-text-block__heading-3">
      <!---->About AI quality and learning<!---->
<!---->    </h3>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember384" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <strong><!---->“How do you measure whether the AI output is getting better over time?”<!----></strong><span class="white-space-pre"> </span>AI systems that don’t improve are failing to capture their main advantage.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember385" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <strong><!---->“What data are you collecting to improve the system?”<!----></strong><span class="white-space-pre"> </span>Should include both usage patterns and explicit user feedback.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember386" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <strong><!---->“Can you demonstrate the system learning from user corrections?”<!----></strong><span class="white-space-pre"> </span>The feedback loop should be observable and measurable.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <h3 id="ember387" class="ember-view reader-text-block__heading-3">
      <!---->About operational maturity<!---->
<!---->    </h3>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember388" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <strong><!---->“How long does setup take for a new team member?”<!----></strong><span class="white-space-pre"> </span>Anything over 30 minutes suggests operational complexity that will slow scaling.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember389" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <strong><!---->“What happens if the system fails during a demo?”<!----></strong><span class="white-space-pre"> </span>Tests whether they have fallback plans and error handling.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember390" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <strong><!---->“Can you show me the system working end-to-end with real data?”<!----></strong><span class="white-space-pre"> </span>Distinguishes between proof-of-concept and production-ready systems.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <h3 id="ember391" class="ember-view reader-text-block__heading-3">
      <!---->About technical debt<!---->
<!---->    </h3>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember392" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <strong><!---->“What shortcuts have you taken that need to be addressed?”<!----></strong><span class="white-space-pre"> </span>Honest teams will have a list; dishonest ones will claim there are none.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember393" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <strong><!---->“Where are you most likely to hit scaling problems?”<!----></strong><span class="white-space-pre"> </span>Reveals understanding of system limitations and growth challenges.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember394" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <strong><!---->“What would break if we doubled our user base tomorrow?”<!----></strong><span class="white-space-pre"> </span>Tests whether the system is built for real-world usage patterns.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <h2 id="ember395" class="ember-view reader-text-block__heading-2">
      <!---->Red flags for product leaders<!---->
<!---->    </h2>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember396" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <strong><!---->Architecture Drift<!----></strong><!---->: If your team frequently says “we’ll need to refactor this later” or “this is just temporary,” you’re accumulating technical debt faster than feature value.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember397" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <strong><!---->Integration Fragility<!----></strong><!---->: If adding new features consistently breaks existing ones, your system lacks proper architectural boundaries. This is especially problematic in AI systems where components are more interconnected.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember398" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <strong><!---->Poor Error Handling<!----></strong><!---->: If the system fails silently or provides cryptic error messages, it’s not ready for real users. AI systems should gracefully explain what went wrong and suggest fixes.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember399" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <strong><!---->Inability to Demonstrate End-to-End Workflows<!----></strong><!---->: If your team can only demo individual components rather than complete user journeys, the integration work isn’t complete.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember400" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <strong><!---->No Learning Mechanism<!----></strong><!---->: If the AI doesn’t improve from user interactions, you’re missing the main value proposition of AI systems.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <h2 id="ember401" class="ember-view reader-text-block__heading-2">
      <!---->The bottom line for product leaders<!---->
<!---->    </h2>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember402" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <!---->Building AI products requires the same product discipline as any other complex system: clear requirements, consistent user experience, and sustainable technical foundation. The difference is that AI systems must also learn and improve over time, which adds complexity that traditional product development doesn’t face.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember403" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <strong><!---->The most successful AI products aren’t those with the most sophisticated algorithms — they’re those with solid product foundations that enable the AI to improve through real use.<!----></strong>
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember404" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <!---->As a product leader, your job is ensuring your technical team builds that foundation rather than getting caught up in AI capabilities that can’t deliver sustained value to users.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember405" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <strong><!---->The counterintuitive insight<!----></strong><!---->: The best AI products often look boring from a technical perspective because they’ve invested in the unsexy work of operational reliability, consistent user experience, and systematic improvement processes.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <h2 id="ember406" class="ember-view reader-text-block__heading-2">
      <!---->What I’ve learned about AI product management<!---->
<!---->    </h2>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember407" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <!---->Traditional product management focuses on understanding user needs and coordinating development to meet them. AI product management adds a layer: understanding how AI systems learn and ensuring that learning aligns with user value.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember408" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <strong><!---->The key difference<!----></strong><!---->: Traditional products get better through updates. AI products get better through use. This means product managers need to design for learning, not just functionality.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember409" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <strong><!---->The systematic insight<!----></strong><!---->: The same discipline that creates good traditional products — clear requirements, consistent architecture, user-centered design — creates the foundation for AI systems that can actually improve over time.<!---->
<!---->    </p>



<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->
<!---->

    <p id="ember410" class="ember-view reader-text-block__paragraph">
      <!---->Sometimes the most important product management skill for AI projects is knowing when to slow down and fix the foundation rather than rushing to add more impressive features.<!---->
<!---->    </p>
