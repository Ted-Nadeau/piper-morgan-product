## The Opportunity

Steve Yegge just published a 25-page article describing "Gas Town," his multi-agent orchestration system. Despite being dense and technical, the article is readable, memorable, and has generated significant attention.

The reason it works: he crystallized complex systems into named concepts with personality (GUPP, MEOW, Polecats, Convoys), provided clear diagrams, offered a maturity model for readers to locate themselves, and made his philosophy explicit.

We have equally sophisticated methodology. We don't have equally compelling articulation.

**Proposed initiative**: Collaborate on a "Piper Morgan Methodology Guide" that tells our story as effectively as Yegge tells his.

### Named Concepts (need polish, not creation)

|---------|-----------|--------|

| Building-in-Public | Transparency as accountability mechanism and methodology laboratory | Active practice |

### Philosophy We've Made Explicit

- "Methodology IS the speed optimization" — systematic approaches that initially seem slower actually accelerate by preventing rework
- Cathedral vs. Bazaar quality — we're building something that needs to last
- "Fish fall out of the barrel" (Yegge) vs. "No work lost" (us) — fundamentally different optimization targets
- Verification theater is the anti-pattern — tests that pass without validating functionality

### What We Lack

1. **A unified document** — concepts scattered across ADRs, methodology docs, session logs
2. **Visual representation** — no diagrams, no illustrations
3. **A maturity model** — what are the stages of adopting verification-first methodology?
4. **The compression** — Yegge fits a complex system into ~25 pages with personality

## Proposed Structure

### Part 1: The Problem We're Solving
- The "75% pattern" (work abandoned at 75-80% completion)
- Verification theater (green tests, broken software)
- Context loss across sessions and agents
- Assumption cascades that kill productivity

### Part 2: Core Principles
- Verification-First (with the K8s/Gas Town reframe: "Is it done?" not "Is it running?")
- Completion Discipline (the Triad explained)
- Systematic > Fast (methodology IS speed)
- Human-Orchestrated AI (not autonomous, not manual—something new)

### Part 3: The Excellence Flywheel
- What it is
- How it compounds
- How to know if it's working
- Visual representation (need illustration)

### Part 4: The Inchworm Protocol
- The discipline
- Why 100% completion matters
- What happens when you skip it
- Visual representation (need illustration)

### Part 5: Multi-Agent Coordination
- Role specialization
- Structured handoffs
- Session logs as institutional memory
- Evidence-based completion
- Visual representation (need illustration—maybe contrast with Yegge's chaos)

### Part 6: Maturity Model (New)
We need to create this. Rough sketch:

| Stage | Behavior | Characteristics |
|-------|----------|-----------------|
| 1 | No AI | Traditional development |
| 2 | AI as tool | Chat-based assistance, manual integration |
| 3 | AI as colleague | Structured roles, ad-hoc coordination |
| 4 | Systematic AI | Verification protocols, completion discipline |
| 5 | Multi-agent orchestration | Specialized roles, human-orchestrated handoffs |
| 6 | Methodology-as-product | Process generates reusable patterns |

### Part 7: Honest About Tradeoffs
- Slower on day 1, faster on day 100
- Requires discipline that feels bureaucratic until it compounds
- Human orchestration won't scale to 30 agents (unlike Yegge)
- We optimize for quality, not throughput
